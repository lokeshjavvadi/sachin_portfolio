Organisational Behaviour and Leadership: Kotter's 8-Step Change Model

In the module of Organisational Behaviour, the concept of Kotter 8-Step Change Model was one of the most enlightening frameworks I worked on. The framework provides a step-by-step guide to execute effective organisational change, which begins by generating the sense of urgency and concludes with cultural entrenchment of the new practices.

The point that caught my attention was that early buy-in and the importance of maintaining and supporting momentum is critical through having a clear vision and short-term wins.

I used this model in our group assignment on The Warehouse Group (TWG) where we examined the problem of staff disengagement and high turnover. The Kotter framework assisted us in creating a balanced and realistic change plan based on addressing leadership gaps and cultural resistance. For example, we stressed the importance of developing a guiding coalition among middle level managers and implementing visible wins like employee recognition schemes.

Through this experience, I learned how resistance to change can stem from limitations in communication and lack of engagement, not just individual reluctance. Following this systematic procedure helped me understand how to anticipate such problems and create interventions to address them proactively. This model will prove beneficial in future leadership roles, particularly in digital transformation scenarios where resistance often accompanies rapid adjustment periods.

Cross-Cultural Management: Hofstede's Cultural Dimensions Theory

I gained important knowledge about the role national culture plays in determining workplace behaviour during our module on cross-cultural management when we studied Hofstede's Cultural Dimensions, which included power distance, individualism vs collectivism, and uncertainty avoidance.

I now understand that assumptions, values, and communication methods can vary significantly across cultures and that these differences can either benefit or complicate team collaboration.

This learning was especially useful in a cross-cultural project team when a misunderstanding arose between culturally diverse team members resulting in tension. Some teammates preferred a direct approach and swift decision-making while others valued consensus and hierarchical approval. Through research considering Hofstede's dimensions, I understood these discrepancies were cultural rather than personal. Based on this insight, we modified our communication approach—encouraging open dialogue while respecting all members' cultural sensitivities.

This reflection also stressed the value of cultural intelligence (CQ) in today's diverse work climate. It reminded me that inclusive leadership isn't about treating everyone equally, but about appreciating and capitalizing on our cultural differences to build more cohesive and productive teams. I plan to continue developing this ability, particularly as I anticipate working in international business strategy or consulting roles.

Business Ethics and Sustainability: Triple Bottom Line (People, Planet, Profit)

The introduction of the Triple Bottom Line (TBL) concept during business ethics classes significantly impacted my vision of corporate success. This model emphasizes that companies should evaluate performance not just by profit, but also by their treatment of people and the planet.

This challenged my previous belief that profitability and ethics were mutually exclusive, as I learned that ethical and sustainable practices can create long-term value.

I applied this framework in analyzing a retail company accused of greenwashing—exaggerating their environmental friendliness claims. Using the TBL model, we assessed their performance in social responsibility, environmental sustainability, and economic transparency. This revealed discrepancies between their branding and actual operations, allowing us to recommend ethical improvements like transparent reporting and stakeholder consultation.

This reflection strengthened my commitment to incorporating sustainability into future business strategies. Considering my operational experience and potential future consultancy, I want to advance business models that are not only profitable but socially responsible. Ethics and sustainability are no longer optional—they're essential for building consumer trust, employee loyalty, and regulatory compliance.
